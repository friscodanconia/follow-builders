OpenAI just explained why GPT-5 started talking like a fantasy character, while everyone else is still arguing about whether AI will create or destroy software jobs. One of these conversations actually matters.

**OpenAI reveals the "goblin problem" behind GPT-5's personality quirks**
The company published a detailed post-mortem explaining how GPT-5 developed what users called "goblin outputs" — personality-driven responses that made the AI sound like it was roleplaying a fantasy character. The technical breakdown covers the timeline of how these behaviors spread through the model, the root cause, and the fixes they implemented.
Why it matters: This is the first time a major lab has pulled back the curtain on how AI personalities emerge and spread. Every company building on top of these models needs to understand that weird behaviors aren't just bugs — they're contagious patterns that can infect an entire deployment.
https://openai.com/index/where-the-goblins-came-from

**Box CEO pushes back on the "AI will kill coding jobs" narrative**
Aaron Levie argued that software jobs aren't disappearing — they're about to explode. "Agents are the single biggest form of leverage for anyone technical in history," he said, predicting that AI will create demand for "100X more software than before" rather than just reducing work from an existing pie.
Why it matters: While VCs tweet about AI replacing developers, one of enterprise software's most successful CEOs is telling his customers the opposite. If Levie is right, companies laying off engineers to "prepare for AI" are making the same mistake as those who cut web developers in 1995.

**Developer deploys AI code review on every commit**
Peter Steinberger shared that he's now automatically spinning up an AI instance to review every code commit for regressions and security issues. The system runs for 10 minutes per commit and has already caught bugs he missed.
Why it matters: This is AI code review moving from "nice to have" to "always on." When a solo developer can afford to run AI security audits on every single change, the excuse for shipping vulnerable code just got a lot thinner.

**The great CPU shortage is coming for AI inference**
Latent Space Newsletter connected the dots on a brewing infrastructure crisis. While everyone focused on GPU shortages for training, the real bottleneck is emerging in CPU compute for running AI applications. Intel's CEO noted rising demand, and industry analysts point out that companies spent all their hardware budgets on GPUs while neglecting the CPU infrastructure needed to actually run the AI they're building.
Why it matters: Your company's AI strategy probably assumes infinite cheap compute for inference. That assumption is about to get expensive. The same CFOs who approved unlimited ChatGPT licenses are going to start asking hard questions about compute costs per user.
