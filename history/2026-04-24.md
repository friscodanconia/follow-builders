**AI Builders Digest — April 24, 2026**

Google just made Replit Agent an enterprise feature. While everyone else is building standalone AI tools, the smart money is on whoever gets their AI baked into the software people already use every day.

**Google integrates Replit Agent into Gemini Enterprise**
Replit CEO Amjad Masad announced that companies can now call Replit Agent directly from Google's Gemini Enterprise platform. Instead of switching between tools, enterprise users can access Replit's coding agent without leaving their Google workspace.
Why it matters: This is how AI tools actually win in enterprise — not as standalone products, but as features inside the software your IT department already approved. Expect every productivity platform to start hunting for AI integrations like this.

**AI coding agents can now work for hours without human help**
Zara Zhang highlighted a demo showing coding agents that can handle long-running tasks completely autonomously, without requiring human intervention or guidance throughout the process. The setup allows agents to work through complex coding projects from start to finish independently.
Why it matters: Your junior developers just got competition that doesn't need coffee breaks. If agents can handle multi-hour coding tasks solo, the bottleneck shifts from "can AI write code?" to "can humans review what AI built?"

**Y Combinator's Garry Tan: Build bigger AI skills, not more small ones**
Y Combinator President Garry Tan shared his approach to AI skill composition, advocating for combining adjacent capabilities into larger, more flexible skills with branching parameters rather than maintaining separate small skills. He's finding that consolidating related functions into comprehensive tools works better than keeping everything modular.
Why it matters: If you're building AI workflows with dozens of tiny, specialized prompts, you're probably doing it wrong. The VCs funding AI startups are betting on consolidation over fragmentation.
https://x.com/garrytan/status/2047183884266402275

**OpenAI shares 10 practical Codex use cases for work**
OpenAI published a guide detailing 10 real-world applications for Codex in workplace settings, focusing on automating tasks, creating deliverables, and transforming inputs into outputs across different tools and workflows.
Why it matters: OpenAI is moving from "here's what Codex can do" to "here's how your company should actually use it." That's usually the signal that a tool is ready for mainstream business adoption.
https://openai.com/academy/top-10-use-cases-codex-for-work

**The AI "overcooking" problem is real**
Ryo Lu warned about "overcooking" in AI-powered product development — when teams add every possible feature because AI makes building cheap. The result: dashboards with sparklines on every number, confirmation modals for every action, and animated illustrations for every empty state. Each decision makes sense individually, but together they create chaos.
Why it matters: Your product team is about to ship twice as many features this quarter thanks to AI assistance. Most of them will make your product worse, not better. Someone needs to start saying no.
